🧠 Weekly Big Ideas
›

1. Task-level exposure, not job-level replacement

Frey & Osborne's reappraisal shifts the frame from "will this job disappear" to "which specific tasks within this job are exposed to automation." That distinction matters — most jobs are a bundle of tasks with very different exposure levels, which is exactly what Activity 1 asks you to map.

2. Creative labor needs new governance categories

Consent, credit, and compensation weren't designed for a world where models train on creative work at scale. The CHI paper argues existing frameworks are structurally unable to answer basic questions about attribution and fair use in AI-generated creative output.

3. Collective action is a live governance tool

The WGA strike didn't just win contract terms — it demonstrated that organized labor can set enforceable boundaries on AI use in creative production, in real time, without waiting for regulation.

4. Productivity gains are not guaranteed or evenly distributed

The OECD report frames AI's productivity effects as conditional — dependent on adoption patterns, complementary skills, and firm-level capacity to actually use the tools well. It's not an automatic uplift.

5. "Workslop" is a new failure mode, not just low quality

HBR's term describes AI-generated content that looks finished but isn't — it shifts the burden of real work downstream to whoever has to fix it, actually reducing net productivity even as individual output volume rises.

6. The future of work is being negotiated, not predicted

Across every source this week — strikes, governance papers, policy reports — the throughline is that "the future of work" isn't a fixed outcome technology determines. It's an ongoing negotiation between labor, capital, and platforms, and creator economy work is squarely inside that negotiation.

📓 Master Notes
›
Use concept-first notes, not source-by-source notes. Six sections organized by idea, matching the week's arc from labor economics to platform-level implications.

1. Task-level exposure and the occupational framework

  • Core point: Frey & Osborne's reappraisal reframes automation risk at the task level, not the job level. This is the direct methodology behind Activity 1's ONet exercise.
  • Key terms: task exposure, automation risk, occupational analysis, ONet, Tasks vs. Work Activities.
  • Key source connections: Frey & Osborne reappraisal; Activity 1 ONet Occupation Analysis.
  • Use for Activity 1: This is the theoretical grounding for why you're rating individual tasks rather than declaring a whole occupation "safe" or "at risk."

2. Creative-labor governance: consent, credit, compensation

  • Core point: The CHI paper argues that existing IP and labor frameworks don't have good answers for AI training on creative work — consent is rarely obtained, credit is rarely given, and compensation mechanisms barely exist.
  • Key terms: consent, credit, compensation, creative labor governance, training data provenance.
  • Key source connections: Governance of Generative AI in Creative Work (CHI 2025).
  • Use for memo: Strong angle if you want to connect governance gaps to platform responsibility for creator content.

3. Collective action as governance: the WGA strike

  • Core point: The Hollywood writers' strike shows organized labor successfully negotiating binding AI-use protections directly with employers — a real-time governance mechanism that doesn't require legislation.
  • Key terms: collective bargaining, AI-use protections, labor organizing, contract enforcement.
  • Key source connections: Brookings: Hollywood writers went on strike.
  • Use for memo: Strong personal connection if you want to explore what equivalent protections could look like for creators on platforms without unions.

4. Macro productivity and entrepreneurship effects

  • Core point: The OECD report frames AI's economic effects as conditional on adoption quality, not automatic — productivity gains depend on complementary skills and organizational readiness, not just access to the tools.
  • Key terms: productivity paradox, adoption readiness, complementary skills, entrepreneurship effects.
  • Key source connections: OECD AI Papers 2025.
  • Use for memo: Good if you want a macro/policy angle rather than a labor-specific one.

5. Workslop: a new productivity failure mode

  • Core point: AI-generated content that looks complete but requires significant rework shifts effort downstream rather than eliminating it — net productivity can fall even as visible output rises.
  • Key terms: workslop, downstream rework, apparent vs. actual productivity.
  • Key source connections: HBR: AI-Generated "Workslop" Is Destroying Productivity.
  • Use for memo: Sharp, concrete counterpoint if your angle is skeptical of AI productivity claims.

6. Podcast synthesis: Shell Game and the human stakes

  • Core point: Shell Game (S2) puts human faces and stories on the abstract labor-market and governance questions from the readings — use it to find concrete examples for your memo rather than treating it as separate material.
  • Key terms: (episode-specific — note as you listen).
  • Key source connections: All 8 episodes, cross-referenced against the readings above.
  • Use for memo: A moment from the podcast paired with a claim from one of the readings is often the strongest kind of Week 4 memo evidence.

Angle 3: Task-level exposure is a better frame than job-level fear
