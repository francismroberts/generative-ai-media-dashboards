Activity 1: NotebookLM Podcast critique 🎙️

Official instructions: Choose one reading from this week or prior weeks. Go to NotebookLM, upload the reading, and create an Audio Overview. Listen to the podcast and compare it to your own interpretation of the reading. Critique the podcast for key information it missed, validity or accuracy concerns, comprehensiveness, and other relevant issues. Post your critique to the designated Canvas discussion thread.

Recommended readings for Activity 1:

  • Guardian: Brands using AI-generated influencers — strong if you want to critique a source where you have practitioner context
  • LA Times: Washed Out and Sora — good if you want to critique a creative/production-focused piece
  • McKinsey: How generative AI can boost consumer marketing — useful if you want to build your Assignment 2 understanding while completing Activity 1
  • Brennan Center: Generative AI in Political Advertising — strong if you want to critique a policy/governance source
  • MIT Technology Review: How generative AI could reinvent play — good if you want a distinctive topic away from the main memo cluster

Critique framework:

  • Key information missed: What important nuances, caveats, or counterarguments did the podcast omit?
  • Validity or accuracy: Did the podcast misrepresent any claims, overstate conclusions, or introduce inaccuracies?
  • Comprehensiveness: How well did it cover the full scope of the original reading?
  • Other aspects: Tone, framing, accessibility, what it added that wasn't in the text.

Estimated effort: 60 minutes (includes upload, generation, listening, note-taking, and drafting the post).

Activity 2: Vibe Coding 💻

Official instructions: Design and vibe code a functioning information tool using Gemini Canvas. Brainstorm tool ideas, narrow to one, define the problem and audience. Answer the 5 PRD questions below. Prompt Gemini Canvas with your PRD, sketches/mockups, and any needed data (synthetic is fine). Test and iterate. Post a link to your tool, your PRD, and a reflection to the Canvas discussion thread.

PRD questions (answer these before building):

  1. Why are we building this?
  2. Who is it for?
  3. How does it accomplish the goal for the user?
  4. What does it look like and how does the user interact with it?
  5. What data is needed to prototype the experience?

Recommended tool ideas:

  • Assignment 2 production tracker — track videos, tools used, iteration notes, submission status
  • Grad school reading dashboard — track readings, completion status, memo angle notes by week
  • Creator campaign planning tool — product, segment, message, platform, format in one view
  • AI disclosure decision tree — a tool that walks through when and how to disclose AI use
  • Meal prep tracker or personal productivity tool — build something you'll actually use

Estimated effort: 120 minutes (PRD: 30m, first build: 45m, iteration + testing: 30m, reflection post: 15m). Do the PRD first — clarity here saves significant iteration time.

🧠 Weekly Big Ideas
›

1. AI is becoming a media production layer

The shift in Week 3 is not about AI helping humans write — it's about AI generating the media itself. Video, voice, code, data visualizations, synthetic performers. This is a structural change in who can produce professional-quality media and at what cost.

2. Every domain creates a different trust problem

In journalism, the risk is accuracy and attribution. In marketing, it's misleading personalization. In Hollywood, it's likeness and labor. In politics, it's manipulation and microtargeting. The tool is the same; the trust failure modes are specific to the domain.

3. Synthetic creators challenge the influencer model

AI-generated influencers look real, scale infinitely, never go off-brand, and don't demand residuals. Brands love them. Platforms have to decide what disclosure looks like when the "creator" is a generated persona, not a person.

4. Supervision is the core skill

Whether you're vibe coding, analyzing data with ChatGPT, or prompting Sora for video frames — the high-value skill is not generating output, it's evaluating, verifying, and knowing when to override. Human judgment remains the critical layer.

5. Voice and likeness are the highest-stakes consent issues

ElevenLabs can clone a voice from a short sample. CBS shows AI-generated performers appearing in ads without explicit per-use consent. The Brennan Center shows AI voice used in political ads. Consent frameworks have not caught up to the technology.

6. AI in games may be the most transformative long-term shift

Moving NPCs from scripted dialogue trees to open-ended AI-generated responses changes what a game is — from a fixed authored experience to an adaptive, interactive world that responds to every player differently. The storytelling and trust implications extend far beyond games.

📓 Master Notes
›
Use concept-first notes, not source-by-source notes. Six sections — organized by idea, not reading order. Each section maps to sources, key terms, and how it feeds the memo, activities, or Assignment 2.

1. GenAI as media-production infrastructure

  • Core point: AI has moved from a writing assistant to a full production layer — generating text, image, video, audio, music, data analysis, code, and interactive experiences at scale. Week 3 is about use cases across domains and modalities, not about how AI works internally.
  • Key terms: production layer, AI-generated media, modality, domain use case, transformation, personalization, distribution.
  • Key source connections: Week 3 lecture (domain/modality map), GAIN newsroom taxonomy (production, transformation, distribution), LA Times / Sora (video), ElevenLabs video tutorial (audio/voice), Data Analysis with ChatGPT (data), Vibe Coding for Newsrooms (code), MIT Tech Review (interactive).
  • Why it matters: This is the frame for the whole week. Every other section is a specific application of AI-as-production-layer in a different domain.
  • Use for memo: The strongest Week 3 memo angle positions AI not as a tool but as infrastructure — and asks what governance follows from that.

2. Newsroom, data, and tool-building workflows

  • Core point: GAIN documents seven newsroom AI applications (discovery, gathering, production, transformation, management, distribution, interaction). Data analysis and vibe coding extend this into analytical and development workflows — but human review, verification, and supervision remain the critical skill layer in all three.
  • Key terms: newsroom AI, data supervision, code-checking, prompt specificity, version control, vibe coding, PRD, Gemini Canvas.
  • Key source connections: GAIN: Keeping Track of AI Use Cases in the Newsroom; Data Analysis with ChatGPT; Paul Bradshaw on AI tools for data analysis; GAIN: Vibe Coding for Newsrooms.
  • Why it matters: Supervision is the differentiating skill — not generation. In all three workflow types, the human must verify outputs, catch failures, and know when to override.
  • Use for Activity 2: Read Vibe Coding for Newsrooms, then draft your PRD before opening Gemini Canvas. Clarity on what you're building saves iteration time.

3. Marketing, advertising, and persuasion

  • Core point: McKinsey covers AI for personalization, segmentation, campaign generation, creative testing, and workflow automation — with guardrails as a necessary counterweight. The Brennan Center shows the persuasion risk when AI-generated targeting, synthetic voices, and microtargeting are applied to political advertising at scale.
  • Key terms: personalization, segmentation, AI creative testing, guardrails, political microtargeting, deepfake advertising, persuasion risk, disclosure requirements.
  • Key source connections: McKinsey: How generative AI can boost consumer marketing; Brennan Center: Generative AI in Political Advertising.
  • Why it matters: Marketing and political advertising represent the two ends of the persuasion risk spectrum — one commercially motivated, one civically consequential.
  • Use for Assignment 2: McKinsey gives the segmentation and campaign framework. Two audience segments, two short social videos, one write-up explaining platform, angle, tools, and reflection.

4. Synthetic media and synthetic people

  • Core point: AI is generating not just content but people — synthetic performers appearing in ads (CBS / Tilly Norwood), AI-generated influencer personas operating on brand deals (Guardian), AI-generated music videos (LA Times / Sora), and voice clones that can replicate any speaker from a short sample (ElevenLabs). Consent, likeness, labor, and disclosure frameworks have not kept pace.
  • Key terms: synthetic performer, AI-generated influencer, synthetic persona, UGC-style content, voice cloning, dubbing, likeness rights, consent, labor displacement, disclosure.
  • Key source connections: LA Times / Washed Out / Sora; CBS / Tilly Norwood / AI actors; Guardian / AI-generated influencers; ElevenLabs voice/audio video tutorial.
  • Why it matters: Synthetic people are both a creative production capability and a trust and governance problem — for audiences, platforms, and regulators simultaneously.
  • Use for memo: This is the strongest source cluster for the Week 3 memo. Platform responsibility for disclosure and monetization of synthetic creators sits directly in your Snapchat lane.

5. Games, play, and interactive media

  • Core point: AI-generated NPCs, adaptive world-building, and unscripted dialogue are moving games from authored fixed experiences to open-ended interactive worlds that respond differently to every player. The implications extend beyond games into any interactive media where simulated characters develop ongoing relationships with users.
